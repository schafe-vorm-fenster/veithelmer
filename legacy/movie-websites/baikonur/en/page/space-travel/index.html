About the Baikonur Cosmodrome

May 7, 2010 – 17:28

Baikonur is the world’s first and largest space launch facility, located on the desert steppes of Kazakhstan about 200 kilometers east of the Aral Sea. Built by the Soviet Union in the late 1950s as part of its ambitious space program, the Cosmodrome extends for 90 km from north to south and for 120 km east to west. The name ‘Baikonur’ was chosen to misdirect the West to Baikonur, a mining town about 300 kilometers northeast of the launch center.
 

The expense of constructing launch facilities and several hundred kilometers of new road and train lines made the Cosmodrome one of the most costly infrastructure projects the Soviets ever undertook.

A town was built around the facility to provide housing, schools and support infrastructure for 100.000 workers. It was named Leninsk.

Many historic flights lifted off from Baikonur: the first satellite, Sputnik, in 1957; the first satellite to travel to the Moon, Luna, in 1959; the first man in space, Yuri Gagarin, 1961; and the first woman in space, Valentina Tereshkova, in 1963. In 1995, the city surrounding the spaceport was renamed Baikonur.

When the United States’ Space Shuttle fleet was grounded following the Columbia Disaster in 2003, Baikonur was the only gateway to the International Space Station.

Baikonur is leased by the Kazakh government to Russia (until 2050) and is managed by the Russian Federal Space Agency at a cost of 115 million US dollars per year. During their decline in the years after the dissolution of the Soviet Union, the Russian Space Agency started selling the third seat in the Soyuz rocket to private space tourists.

Downrange from the launch pad, spent launch equipment drops directly onto the ground – where it is salvaged by Space Agency workers and by the local population.

3 Astronauts on their way to Soyuz in Baikonur

May 6, 2010 – 23:51

On May 27th 2009, three Astronauts, Belgian Frank de Winne, Russian Roman Romanenko and Canadian Robert Thirsk, embarqued the rocket Soyuz for a 188-days long trip in space. Veit Helmer was there and documented them on their way to the rocket, that was launched later that day.
