Diary

Production diary

November 24, 2010 – 22:46

We left the hotel at 7 am. We arrived at the hangar shortly after, as we stay in a hotel near the airport. Marie got an injection against motion sickness. Unfortunately it made her sleepy for 30 minutes and I could not tell her last directions. At 9 am Novespace kicked me out of the plane and closed the doors. Then the plane took off for four hours! It made 31 parabolas. I was very nervous, waiting on the ground. After half of the flight, I got the message, that Marie is fine. My biggest fear, that Marie might get sick, did not happen. At 12.50 h, the plane landet. There were many scientist on board, then Marie stepped out. She was happy and exited. Also Michael Müller from DLR seemed to be satisfied, he played a cosmonaut in the scene! They shot all according to the schedule, expect the scene with the brush.
Unfortunately the film did not last for all parabolas. The crew from Novespace was really helpful during the shooting, Marie told me. So thank you Christophe, Stephane and Frederic and also staff from DLR!
The Airbus 300 Zero G made its 10000 parabola today and Marie got a medal, that she was part of that flight!

Production diary

November 22, 2010 – 23:45

We are allowed to shoot inside the Zero G-plane of Novespace. Thanks to the Geman Space Agency DLR! We arrived today in Bordeaux. Marie de Villepin, my DOP Nikolai and make-up-artist Aya Yabuuchi.
Actually only Marie is allowed to be inside the plane. We are provided with a special cameramen, who is experienced to shoot in zero gravity, Pierre Weller.
At 14 h was the briefing. I thought weightlessness appears, when the plane has its nose down, but actually it is the opposite! When the plane has its nose up, it will suddenly stop the climb and during the following parabola there will be no gravity for 22 seconds!
We brought a 35mm camera. It is the first time, such a camera is inside this plane. But they approved it!
The crew is cool, the plane looks great, I am really exited. Marie also! She is cool and has no fear at all!

Production diary

November 2, 2010 – 20:50

Our second day in Moscow.
We shot at the centrifuge. I am unable to describe this place, one has too google a picture in the web.
It is a giant hammer, spinning in a huge round room, and inside that cosmonauts are tested for their ability to fly to space. We attached the camera to the centrifuge with a special manufactured grip mount, as the forces of the centrifuge are HUGE, and it was a big danger, that the camera just would splash against the wall!!!
We spent more hours to take amazing shots from every angle and then shot the scenes how Gagarin is being taken inside the centrifuge and one more scene Julie is taken out of that thing!
The workers from the centrifuge were really helpful!

Research

July 9, 2010 – 16:00

Sergej did a great job researching space travel through books and the internet, but it lacked a feeling of realism.
Since working with Gordan Mihic, the screenwriter of such fantastic films as “Time of the Gypsies” and “Black Cat, white Cat”, I know about the importance of investigating reality before embarking into a fairy tale.
Sergej and I needed to see the place where rockets are launched.
A trip to space costs 20 Mio. USD (or 35 Mio. USD, if you travel two weeks instead of one).
For a trip to see a space launch at Baikonur, travel agents in the internet asked for 4000 Euros. I contacted a travel agent in Kazakhstan and booked two trips for half the price. Still quite expensive, but I felt necessary.
Another option would have been to ask for an official press invitation. But I felt i was not ready yet, to answer the inevitable questions about the project. I wanted to visit Baikonur rather as a tourist.
On May 25, 2009 Sergej and I travelled by plane from Berlin via Istanbul, Almaty to Kzyl-Orda. We then were supposed to take a train to Baikonur. As Sergej recalled numerous bad experiences of travelling by train in former Soviet countries, he asked the driver who took us from the airport to the train station to continue the ride for four more hours (and 80 dollars) and we reached Baikonur.
Baikonur is in the middle of nowhere. It was a former secret city, not indicated on official maps. And still today, you need a special permit to enter the city. The local contact man was waiting for us at the gate. Soldiers were checking the cars and papers. After 40 hours of travelling, we finally arrived.
Our hotel had little comfort. But we did not complain, as our mission was to work, not to take a vacation.
Every morning at 8 am, a car came to pick us up and brought us to the space station, around 30 kilometres north of the city. The workers had already left at 6 am with seven trains, bringing them to the different platforms.
The rockets are built in Russia and assembled and launched in Baikonur. The assembly halls looked less spectacular than I imagined. Our tour included many memorial sites and museums. I started to worry, what places to use as locations for the film.
We tried as much as possible to see places off the beaten track and to speak with workers and inhabitants of Baikonur about their daily life.
On our last day in Baikonur, a Soyuz TMA rocket started with three cosmonauts. Thanks to our new friends from the press department, Sergej and I were allowed to be in the VIP-department, when the cosmonauts were getting the permission to go. The experience to see a rocket taking off was unique and unforgettable.

Idea of Baikonur

May 10, 2010 – 17:00

I cannot remember exactly when I had the idea to shoot a movie about Baikonur.
For many years, newspaper clips and pictures about Cosmonauts have attracted my attention.

But a location is not a story…

When I was sound-mixing my last movie “Absurdistan”, the assistant sound mixer Alexej asked me if his father Sergej could come visit us the following day. I never met Sergej, but I knew that he is a known screenwriter. I agreed and started to guess about a possible reason for his visit. I could not imagine that he would be interested in watching the sound mix (a rather technical process!) and speculated, that Sergej was keen to write for me. As I had not yet lined up any new project, I saw the opportunity of Sergej and I developing a screenplay together, which could become my fourth feature film. While thinking what to propose to him, I remembered the idea of making a movie about Baikonur. That day, my sound mixers worked long hours. They would tinker about one or two hours for a 15-second scene, to offer me the best of all possible mixes of 80 tracks. To give valuable input, I stayed outside the studio and only went inside once they had achieved a good preliminary result. While sitting outside, instead of counting the minutes, I started researching about Baikonur, preparing for Sergej Ashkenazy’s visit the next day. Google showed me a million results for “Baikonur”. I also tried combinations, such as “Baikonur – accidents”. Terrible stories about the tragic explosion of the Buran space shuttle appeared. I tried “Baikonur – funny” and reports about nearby villages popped up, telling stories about villagers collecting rocket pieces that fall down in the steppes after a rocket starts.

I was struck by this contrast between space tourism and space garbage.

Space tourism…

Five persons have visited the international space station after paying 20 – 30 Million USD. Among them, Microsoft manager Charles Simonyi and Iranian–American business woman Anousheh Ansari.

Space garbage…

When a rocket travels to the orbit, large parts of it fall down to the steppe (propulsion stages). I found a newspaper clip about villagers in Kazakhstan, who collect those parts and build houses with them.

And then there were pictures about cosmonauts returning to earth, landing with their capsules into the steppe. One of those pictures particularly took my attention: A capsule in the steppe that was approached by villagers hours before the rescue team came.

Looking back, all elements of the final story were already there and yet, I was still two treatments and six drafts away from the final screenplay.
When I pitched the idea to Sergej the following day, I found a writer fascinated by space exploration. We agreed that Sergej would write a treatment first, before we entered into contractual obligations.

The story of the film

May 4, 2010 – 12:00

There are two worlds: the world of space, and the world of the steppe. At first we see the former. A rocket is being launched. It will carry three people into orbit, two cosmonauts and one tourist, a young German girl named Rose Dorn. Emitting a mighty roar, the rocket blasts off into the starry, black void.
The village of Kedej, a hundred kilometers from Baikonur. Its buildings are strangely shaped, as if they’ve descended into the desert steppe from a science fiction painting. Silvery titanium cylinders under gold foil roofs and low moon-white cupolas topped with multi-colored striped spires rise above the shimmering surface of a dried-up salt lake. Occasional brown felt yurts darken the landscape. The Kedejans make their living from an unusual business. They search for and collect spent rocket stages, fuel tanks, and spherical booster fairings.

After cleaning off the remnants of fuel, they use them to build houses, garages, and enclosures for their animals. They sell the leftovers to the Chinese. The entire village takes part in the business. Only old people live here. The young have left the village long ago, unless you count Kandrar, the grandson of old Adam, and an eccentric, somber, unsociable girl named Balara. Her dishevelled hair and sloppy, shapeless tunic can often be glimpsed in the village square. The villagers dislike Balara and consider her practically mad.

And now about love. It began when the young Kedejan, Kandrar, caught sight of Rose Dorn, the beautiful space tourist, on television. He liked her very much. And later he also heard her tender voice on the radio (Kandrar was the village radio operator) singing a lullabye from orbit to her little sister in Germany. Kandrar was utterly smitten on the spot. But then tragedy struck. While returning to Earth from orbit, the space tourist and her landing capsule disappeared without a trace. A long search was conducted, but to no avail.

A week later, far into the steppe, Kandrar by pure accident suddenly discovered the lost capsule containing Rose Dorn. She lay in it like Sleeping Beauty, beautiful and still. Kandrar thought she was dead, but soon realized she was asleep in a deep and heavy coma. That same night he brought Rose secretly into his yurt, and hid her there from the neighbors for a long time. He dressed her in his mother’s bridal attire, laid her on a white carpet, and enjoyed the sight of her sleeping day and night, as he burned from unrequited love. But he could not wake her at all. Then a dangerous event occurred that threatened the lives of both Kandrar and his secret captive. To save her, he covered her with his body, placing a fervent kiss upon her rosy lips in a despairing farewell. And along with the roar and shuddering of the earth from the rocket booster that had fallen close to the yurt, his Koketai (meaning tender, as Kandrar had named her) awoke…

Slowly she regained consciousness. At first she remained seated, but in a few days she cautiously stood up. Yet her memory had still not returned. Who was she and where was she from? Koketai could not remember, no matter how she tried. What was this strange dwelling she found herself in? And who was this unfamiliar boy bustling about her? Kandrar told her the whole truth, but Rose didn’t believe it, or more likely she didn’t understand what he was saying. Kandrar told her a lie, that she was his fiancée who had been long been ill, and had forgotten it. More than death he feared that Koketai would leave him if she learned the whole truth. He wouldn’t let her out of the yurt. At night as she slept soundly, he would sit near her, listening to her light breathing, and weep, and whisper words of love, though his virtuous nature kept him from touching her. Love’s yearning possessed Kandrar at other times as well, as when Koketai was washing herself, standing in an old tin washtub with shimmering streams of water flowing from her taut breasts and rounded hips, while he gazed at her through a window in the yurt and even groaned from his overflowing desire…
But everything hidden is eventually revealed. They learned of the girl in the village, and then at the cosmodrome. Kandrar, to save his love, carried Koketai off and hid her in a distant grotto. What happened there is what may always happen between a young boy and girl. The night of love raised Kandrar to the pinnacle of bliss… and Rose’s memory unexpectedly returned. In the morning he went to kiss her, but she politely declined.

Important Russian officials from Baikonur came for her, and her father, the German millionaire Wolfgang Dorn. They took Koketai from Kandrar and brought her back to Germany.

In gratitude for saving Rose, the officials invited Kandrar to work at the cosmodrome. He became a tester of the ejection seats that catapult from the ship in case of emergency. They began launching him into the air, like a guinea pig, up to ten times a day. He’d fly up with smoke and a bang, a sudden silence with Kandrar bobbing in his parachute, surveying the area. It was all beautiful and interesting, but it couldn’t take the place of Koketai for Kandrar; he still loved and waited for her. And she returned to the cosmodrome for a new flight into space. Kandrar prepared a dinner for her, with local food from his village. Rose explained him, that she was going for a long time to understand the strange phenomenon many cosmonauts had seen, that of ghostly human figures floating in space, who were perhaps an unknown extraterrestrial form of life. “What about us?!” exclaimed Kandrar. “What about our night together, Koketai!” Taking his face in her hand, Rose said quietly, “No, Kandrar, no. That wasn’t our night. That night you were with Koketai, and I am not she… I have devoted my love to outer space.”

In the morning she flew away. And Kandrar zoomed in his chair over the spring steppe and whispered, barely audibly, “Farewell…”
He landed in some tall grass, unbuckled his parachute, and without a glance toward the cosmodrome, went into the steppe.
Towards evening he mounted into the steep hills and sat, arranging his legs under him as his ancestors had done for ages. Suddenly he heard a new and tender song. “The Syrtan,” (Bird of Happiness – Kazakh) said Kandrar in amazement.

The prophetic bird, shimmering with magical plumage, was sitting in the branches of a tamarisk bush, and her voice was enchanting.
Someone else was watching Kandrar from a nearby low hill, and also listening to the wondrous bird. She had changed that night. Her hair, combed and gathered back from her forehead, fell onto her shoulders in a soft wave. Her face had acquired beauty and a joyous, luminous peace in the solemn light of the moon. Her perpetually stained tunic had become a marvelous gown. And happiness shone in her eyes.
It was Balara.
